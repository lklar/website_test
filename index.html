Interplanetary Dust Particles

  • Are formed by condensation in the solar nebula, or in the interstellar medium
  • Are typically 0.1 – 1 micrometer in size
  • Are the building blocks of the planets, asteroids, comets, and moons
  • Can be collected in the upper Earth atmosphere, and by spacecraft missions (Rosetta, Stardust)
  • Can be directly observed in cometary dust tails

Pebbles

  • Are formed by the coagulation of the interplanetary dust particles
  • Are typically mm to cm in size
  • Can be observed by Radio Telescopes (ALMA) in other protoplanetary discs
  • Have a porosity of ~65%
  • Have been directly observed on the surface of Comet 67P
  • Clouds of pebbles can collapse under their own gravity which leads to the formation of planetesimals

Planetesimals

  • Planetesimals are ~km-sized objects, which is the critical size to attract material by its own gravity
  • They can be observed today as comets, or asteroids
  • Three different theories exist that explain the formation of planetesimals (Gravitational Collpase, Mass Transfer, or Direct Growth)
  • After their formation, some planetesimals suffered serious alteration by impacts, or temperature changes, while other have been left unchanged until today (comets)
  • Some planetesimals are called “rubble piles”, which means that a catastrophic impact has destroyed the original object, but thanks to gravity, the material reassembled to form a new body, the rubble pile planetesimal
  • Planetesimals are the seeds for the further growth of larger objects in planetary system
